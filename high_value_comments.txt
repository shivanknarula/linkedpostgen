The transition from hydraulics to electric Atlas gave us an interesting jumping point. Grippers are one of the most intricate components of a humanoid robot. They pack tons of functionality. We have to put in a lot of actuation and sensing into a very small space. So it's a very hard design problem. We've taken that long term perspective and tried to maximize learnings through that path. GR1 is a gripper that it's the first gripper of what we call the GR line, where we basically were looking to build something that had the most fundamental and in a way minimalistic capabilities of a human hand. GR one allowed us to learn a ton of what it means to mount a gripper to a humanoid robot that works most of the time well, but sometimes it falls. And inevitably, once in a while, it's gonna fall on top of the gripper. So we wanted to figure out what is a good way to build a gripper that can resist that kind of failure mode, and which is very important to our development path, being able to build grippers that are rugged and reliable. This is our second generation gripper. It has seven degrees of freedom. We have seven different actuators within it. Two for each of the three fingers, and then one for this articulated thumb joint. We have tactile sensing on the fingertips, and we have cameras here in the palm. The whole module is self contained with all the actuation within it. It's very easy to put on and off of the robot. And it was an iteration from our first generation gripper, which had a similar three fingers, but they were there was no thumbs. They're all in the same line. Maybe the most important distinction from GR two to GR one is the addition of a thumb, an opposable thumb. That really expands the type of grasps that it allows us to do. It really increases the flexibility of which objects we can grasp with it. And, really, over the last year or so, we've realized that we can grasp almost anything that we throw at it. We landed on three because we believe that that's the fewest amount of fingers that can achieve very complex manipulation tasks. So the the fact that you do have three fingers brings a lot more to the table over just two because the fact that there's some distance between them can help in maintaining a stable grasp, especially when you're rotating larger or heavier objects that wanna kind of rotate out of your hand. We do have the opportunity with this thumb to do some two finger pinch grasps, can be very helpful, for delicate manipulation of very tiny objects. But that third finger coming into play, when you are grasping something a little heavier or a little larger can be really helpful in in maintaining a stable grasp. We've debated a lot, and we do wonder if there's an opportunity to add more fingers and whether they bring much to the table. We decided not to add more fingers because adding more fingers, it just adds more complexity that if it's not necessary, it always ends up meaning lower reliability, higher cost, just in general, lower speed of development. We shouldn't take that as a dogma though, right? Three fingers was the right answer for the first phases of this program. We will see what is the answer moving forward. Tactile sensing is force feedback. You can think of it as your sense of touch that you have in your own fingers. You can also detect if you've dropped something. So if you didn't mean to, but it's fallen out of your hand, those tactile sensors can give you that feedback to understand what's going on. The goal is to apply as little force as possible, but maintaining a stable grasp. That's very similar to when you pick something up.
